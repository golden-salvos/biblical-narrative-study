## Overview

The previous session traced the broad sweep of Gentile world history through the parallel visions of Daniel 2 and 7 — four empires, rising and falling in sequence, each more brutal than the last, until God's eternal kingdom replaces them all. This session moves from the wide angle to the close-up. Daniel 8 identifies the specific ruler who prefigures the [[Glossary#Antichrist|Antichrist]] and gives a historical event that confirms the prophetic method. Daniel 9 answers a prayer with one of the most precise prophecies in all of Scripture — a countdown, calculable to the day, from a known historical decree to the arrival of the [[Glossary#Messiah|Messiah]] as king. And Revelation 17 takes the sequence of empires and names them, locating the Antichrist's kingdom within a line of seven that runs from Egypt to the present.

---

## 1. Recap: The Four Kingdoms of Daniel 2 and 7

The previous session established the parallel visions of Daniel 2 and 7 — two pictures of the same sequence of Gentile empires, one from the ground up and one from heaven down. Nebuchadnezzar's statue shows humanity's view of its own civilizations: gold, silver, bronze, iron, clay — a monument of diminishing value with impressive feet. Daniel's four beasts show heaven's view of the same powers: predators rising from chaos. The first three kingdoms were identified directly by the text: [[Glossary#Babylon|Babylon]], [[Glossary#Medo-Persia|Medo-Persia]], and [[Glossary#Greece|Greece]]. The fourth — brutal, totalizing, unlike all its predecessors — remains contested by scholars but is presented in this study as a revived [[Glossary#Islamic Caliphate|Islamic Caliphate]]. Both visions end identically: every human empire is swept away and replaced by God's eternal kingdom, given to the [[Glossary#Son of Man|Son of Man]].

Daniel 8 and 9 fill in the gap between that broad sweep and the specific person who embodies the final opposition. They provide both a historical prototype and a precise prophetic timeline.

## 2. Daniel 8: The Ram, the Goat, and the Little Horn

Daniel's vision in chapter 8 is set in the third year of Belshazzar's reign. Daniel sees himself in the citadel of Susa, in the province of Elam — present-day southwestern Iran — standing beside the Ulai Canal.

A ram with two horns appears, one horn longer than the other. The shorter horn was there first; the longer came later and became dominant. The ram charges west, north, and south without restraint, doing as it pleases and becoming great. The angel [[Glossary#Gabriel|Gabriel]] later confirms the identification explicitly: this is [[Glossary#Medo-Persia|Medo-Persia]]. The two horns are its two constituent powers — Media came first and held the greater initial prominence; Persia arose later but ultimately dominated. When Medo-Persia conquered Babylon, it pressed the conquest in all directions, subjugating the known world.

Then a goat appears from the west across the entire earth without touching the ground — a detail conveying extraordinary speed. Between its eyes is a prominent single horn. The goat charges the ram in a rage, shatters both its horns, knocks it to the ground, and tramples it. The goat becomes very great. Then, at the height of its power, the large horn is broken off, and in its place four prominent horns grow up toward the four winds of heaven. Gabriel's interpretation is equally direct: the goat is [[Glossary#Greece|Greece]], the large horn is its first king — historically [[Glossary#Alexander the Great|Alexander the Great]] — and the four horns that replace him are four kingdoms that emerge from his empire but without his power.

> **Key Principle:** God reveals the broad outline to his servants so that when history unfolds exactly as foretold, it produces confirmed faith rather than confusion. The things that were future to Daniel are past to us — and they happened precisely as the vision described.

This is the historical reality: Alexander conquered the known world all the way to India with unmatched speed, died in Babylon in 323 BC without a viable heir, and his empire fractured among his generals, the [[Glossary#Diadochi|Diadochi]]. Four kingdoms eventually stabilized — the northern kingdom centered on Syria and Mesopotamia (the [[Glossary#Seleucid Empire|Seleucid Empire]]), the southern kingdom in Egypt (the [[Glossary#Ptolemaic Empire|Ptolemaic Empire]]), and two others to the east and west. Daniel 11 will focus almost exclusively on the northern and southern kingdoms and their long war, because those two theaters contain the events most relevant to God's people.

Out of one of those four kingdoms — specifically the northern Seleucid kingdom, as later passages confirm — a little horn emerges. It starts small, grows toward the south, east, and toward the "beautiful land" (Jerusalem), and eventually sets itself against "the prince of the host," removes the daily sacrifice, and desecrates the sanctuary.

The little horn has both a near fulfillment and a far one, and understanding this dual pattern is essential to reading Daniel correctly. Something happens in the immediate historical horizon that is a type — a structural preview — of something that will happen on a larger scale in the future. The near fulfillment is [[Glossary#Antiochus IV Epiphanes|Antiochus IV Epiphanes]], who comes to power in 175 BC. He is not royalty by right; he seizes the throne through political intrigue while the legitimate heir is held hostage in Rome. He corrupts the Jerusalem priesthood, enters the temple, sacrifices a pig on the altar of burnt offering — the most egregious defilement possible under Jewish law — and erects an idol of Zeus in the sanctuary. He removes the daily sacrifice and outlaws Jewish religious practice on pain of death.

The Jewish response is a military revolt led by the [[Glossary#Maccabees|Maccabees]] — the priestly family of Mattathias and his sons, most prominently Judas Maccabeus. They recapture Jerusalem, drive out the Seleucid forces, cleanse the temple, and rededicate it. The [[Glossary#Hanukkah|Hanukkah]] festival commemorates that rededication. A voice in the vision asks how long the desolation will last. The answer is 2,300 evenings and mornings. This period, resolved to approximately three and a half years, aligns with the historical interval between Antiochus's desecration and the Maccabean rededication.

> **Key Principle:** Antiochus IV Epiphanes is a type of the Antichrist — not the Antichrist himself. He does in the Greek period what the Antichrist will do at the end of the age: enter the temple, stop the sacrifices, set up an abomination, and declare his supremacy over the God of Israel. The historical event is the pattern. The future event is the fulfillment.

Gabriel's interpretation makes the dual register explicit. He tells Daniel that the vision concerns "the time of the end" and "the appointed time of the end," even while describing events centuries before Christ. The angelic explanation of the stern-faced king who destroys "the mighty and the holy people," takes his stand against "the prince of princes," and is destroyed "not by human power" points beyond Antiochus — Antiochus died of illness in 164 BC, but the figure described here meets his end at the direct intervention of God. Daniel's reaction on receiving all of this is significant: he was exhausted, lay ill for several days, and confessed the vision was beyond his understanding. That is the appropriate response to material that will not be fully legible until the events it describes are unfolding.

## 3. Daniel's Prayer

Daniel 9 opens with Daniel in the first year of Darius the Mede — already transitioned into the second empire of the prophetic sequence. Reading Jeremiah's prophecy, Daniel recognizes that the seventy-year exile is approaching its end (Jeremiah 25:11–12; 29:10). He turns to God in prayer, fasting, and mourning — in sackcloth and ashes.

The prayer is a model of covenantal intercession. Daniel confesses the sins of Israel without minimizing them, works through the complete record of disobedience — from failing to heed the prophets, to transgressing the law of Moses, to refusing to turn back even as the promised curses were poured out — and then appeals not to any merit of Israel's but explicitly to the character of God: *"O Lord, listen! O Lord, forgive! O Lord, hear and act! For your sake, O my God, do not delay, because your city and your people bear your name"* (Daniel 9:19).

The phrase "for your sake" is not rhetorical. Daniel understands, as [[Glossary#Ezekiel|Ezekiel]] makes plain across nearly every chapter of his book, that what drives God to act on behalf of Israel is not Israel's performance but God's own name and reputation among the nations. When God restores the exiles, it will not be because they earned restoration. It will be because the nations watching will thereby know that he is the Lord. Daniel prays within that framework.

His expectation entering the prayer is clear: Jeremiah said seventy years, the seventy years are nearly up, therefore God is about to bring the people home, restore Jerusalem, and set the stage for the coming of the Messiah. That is not what the answer says.

## 4. The Seventy Sevens

Before the prayer is finished, Gabriel arrives in swift flight. As soon as Daniel began to pray, he says, an answer was sent. The first words out of his mouth reframe everything: not seventy years for what Daniel is hoping for, but seventy sevens — seventy times seven, four hundred and ninety years — before six specific things are accomplished.

Those six things are: to finish transgression, to put an end to sin, to atone for wickedness, to bring in everlasting righteousness, to seal up vision and prophecy, and to anoint the Most Holy Place. None of these occurred at the end of the Babylonian exile. None occurred when the temple was rebuilt. They have not all been accomplished yet. They describe the end of the age — the destruction of the Antichrist, the establishment of the [[Glossary#Millennial Kingdom|Millennial Kingdom]], the Messiah enthroned. Daniel was expecting the end of exile. What he received was the entire arc of history compressed into three verses.

> **Key Principle:** Daniel's prayer was answered — but the answer was bigger than the question. The sixty-year exile ended. But the deeper problem the exile exposed — the sin, the rebellion, the fractured relationship between God and his people — required a solution on an entirely different scale.

The four hundred and ninety years are structured in three segments. The starting point is the issuing of a decree to restore and rebuild Jerusalem — identified historically with the decree of Artaxerxes to Nehemiah in 444 BC, recorded in Nehemiah 2:1–8, authorizing the rebuilding of the city walls. The first forty-nine years (seven sevens) account for the physical restoration of Jerusalem, rebuilt "with streets and a trench, but in times of trouble" — the book of Nehemiah documents exactly that, construction happening with a trowel in one hand and a sword in the other. The following four hundred and thirty-four years (sixty-two sevens) run from the completed city to the coming of the Anointed One as ruler. Together, the sixty-nine sevens equal four hundred and eighty-three years.

The mathematics, when adjusted from Jewish lunar years to Julian solar years — a conversion worked through in careful detail by scholar Harold Hoehner — resolve to the year 33 AD. The event that closes the sixty-ninth seven is the [[Glossary#Triumphal Entry|Triumphal Entry]]: Jesus entering Jerusalem on a donkey, 10 Nisan, the day lambs were selected for Passover sacrifice — four days before they were slaughtered on the fourteenth. The crowd spread cloaks and palm branches, crying *Hosanna, blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord.* The alignment is not approximate. It is exact to the day, from a decree issued four hundred and eighty-three years earlier.

> **Key Principle:** The precision of this prophecy is not incidental — it is the point. God gave Daniel a specific countdown to the Messiah's arrival as king, calculable from a known historical event, so that those reading it in hindsight would have no reasonable grounds to deny that Jesus is exactly who he claimed to be.

## 5. The Gap and the Seventieth Seven

After the sixty-ninth seven closes, two events occur that fall outside the prophetic timeline — they happen in a gap between the sixty-ninth and seventieth sevens. First, the Anointed One is cut off and will have nothing (Daniel 9:26). That is the crucifixion — historically dated to Passover of 33 AD, three days after the Triumphal Entry. Second, the people of the ruler who will come will destroy the city and the sanctuary — the destruction of Jerusalem and the temple in 70 AD.

The phrasing of that second event matters. It does not say the ruler will destroy the temple. It says his people will. The ruler himself — the Antichrist — does not appear until the seventieth seven begins. But the people who destroy the temple in 70 AD are ethnically and geographically connected to his future kingdom. The destruction of Jerusalem was carried out by Roman legions under the general Titus, but the legions stationed in Judea were not composed primarily of Italian soldiers. Empires of that scale conscript locally. Historical records indicate Rome did not want the temple destroyed — Titus attempted to stop the fire. The forces that actually set it were predominantly Syrian and Arab conscripts drawn from the local population, the peoples of the northern Seleucid and later Islamic regions. Jesus's statement to his disciples had already specified the outcome precisely: *"not one stone will be left on another"* (Matthew 24:2). As the structure burned, the gold melted into the crevices, and soldiers pried apart every stone to retrieve it.

The gap between the sixty-ninth and seventieth weeks is the [[Glossary#Time of the Gentiles|Time of the Gentiles]] — the period in which Israel's national role in God's redemptive plan is suspended, the gospel reaches the nations, and God's people are drawn from every tribe, tongue, and nation. This gap is not an error in the prophecy. It is a deliberate pause in a specific covenantal timeline, recognized across the New Testament in Isaiah 49, Luke 21:24, Romans 11:25, Galatians 3, and Ephesians 3.

The seventieth seven begins with a specific event: the Antichrist makes a covenant with many for one seven — seven years (Daniel 9:27). This covenant, most likely a peace arrangement centered on Jerusalem and the Middle East, inaugurates the final period of history. At the midpoint — three and a half years in — he breaks the covenant, puts an end to sacrifice and offering, and sets up the [[Glossary#Abomination of Desolation|Abomination of Desolation]] in the temple. For this to occur, two things must be in place before the midpoint: a rebuilt or restored temple on the Temple Mount in Jerusalem, and a functioning sacrificial system. Neither has existed since 70 AD. The seven-year period ends with the destruction of the Antichrist — "the end that is decreed is poured out on him" — which coincides with the return of Jesus and the beginning of the Millennial Kingdom.

## 6. Revelation 17 and the Eight Kings

The session concludes with a turn to Revelation 17:9–11, which John writes approximately six hundred years after Daniel — with the benefit of historical fulfillments Daniel could only see in outline. The passage describes the beast on which the woman rides: seven heads that are both seven hills and seven kings. Five have fallen, one is, one has not yet come. And the beast itself — which once was, now is not, and will come up out of the Abyss — is an eighth king who belongs to the seven.

Working backward from the one certainty: John is writing under Roman rule. Rome is the kingdom that is — the empire in power at the time of writing. That accounts for the sixth king. The five that have fallen are the five Gentile empires that, prior to Rome, each rose to dominance and each came against God's people: Egypt, Assyria, Babylon, Medo-Persia, and Greece. The seventh king — the one who has not yet come and must remain only a little while — is a kingdom that arises after Rome.

The Roman Empire's fragmentation into the Dark Ages in the West left one political-religious power that rose to fill the vacuum across the Middle East, North Africa, and into Spain: the [[Glossary#Islamic Caliphate|Islamic Caliphate]]. At its height it controlled Turkey, all of the Middle East, all of northern Africa, and portions of Europe across the Mediterranean. It was both a theological and political structure — a single caliph holding religious and governmental authority simultaneously, the closest equivalent to the beast's character of any historical institution. It persisted for centuries before being formally disbanded in 1924, when the secular Ottoman government in Turkey exiled the caliph. The seventh king remained for a little while, and then was gone.

The beast who once was, and now is not, and yet will come is the eighth king — the Antichrist. He belongs to the seven because his kingdom is a revived form of one that already existed. He is the Caliphate restored, the Seleucid northern power reanimated, the "king of the North" that Daniel 11 will trace in detail. The current political project of Turkey's leadership — publicly stated as the reconstitution of a unified Islamic political structure under a single authority — is consistent with what this passage anticipates.

> **Key Principle:** The biblical witness on the Antichrist's geographic origin is not a single data point. Daniel 8 says the little horn comes from the Seleucid northern kingdom. Daniel 11 concentrates the prophecy on the king of the North. Isaiah calls the end-times enemy "the Assyrian." Revelation 17 identifies the eighth king as the revival of the seventh kingdom that rose after Rome. The prophets speak with a consistent geographic voice: the region of present-day Turkey and the northern Middle East.

This reading does not require that Rome be irrelevant to prophecy. Rome is the sixth king. What it requires is recognizing that the seventh and eighth are distinct from Rome, that the geographic data in Daniel points to the Middle Eastern north rather than to Europe, and that the conflict being traced here is the same spiritual war that has run from Genesis 3 through every empire on this list — Satan working through earthly kingdoms to eliminate the people and the land through whom God's redemption would and will come. The final chapter of that conflict is what Daniel, Revelation, and the prophets are positioning the reader to understand.
