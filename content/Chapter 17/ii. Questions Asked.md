# Questions Asked — Chapter 17
### The War Behind the War: Daniel 10–12 and Revelation 12–13

*This document records genuine questions raised during the session and the answers given. It is a companion to [[i. The War Behind the War|Chapter 17 — The War Behind the War]], not a replacement for it.*

---

### Q: Why couldn't Gabriel handle the prince of Persia alone — it's just one angel against one angel, isn't it?

The issue is not about raw power. It is about authority. The prince of Persia has legitimate delegated authority over Persia — that territory is his domain. Gabriel's mission involved events inside that domain: specifically, ensuring that the king of Persia would release the Jewish exiles and send them home. The prince of Persia had every reason to resist that outcome, because he governs Persia and what happens inside Persia is within his sphere of authority.

What Gabriel needed was not more strength. He needed authority that matched the domain in question. Michael — the archangel assigned to Israel — carries authority over the Jewish people. The Jews being released were his charge. When Michael entered the contest, the authority of the prince of Israel stood against the authority of the prince of Persia, and Gabriel's mission could proceed. God's will was accomplished not by overpowering the principality but by bringing the correct authority to bear against it.

---

### Q: Do believers have authority over principalities? Can we rebuke them or stop them?

No — not in the way the question implies. Principalities have been given their authority by God. Their power is delegated, bounded, and real. Believers are told to resist the devil and contend against principalities (Ephesians 6:12; James 4:7), but that is different from holding authority to unilaterally revoke what God has assigned.

Daniel 10 makes this concrete. It took the archangel Michael — carrying specific authority over Israel — to check the prince of Persia. If even Gabriel required Michael's authoritative backing to accomplish his mission inside Persian-governed territory, the idea that individual believers can simply command principalities to stand down overstates what Scripture assigns to us. The battle against principalities is real. The weapons are prayer, the Word, the blood of the Lamb, and a life of testimony (Revelation 12:11). Those are not weak weapons. But they operate within the authority structure God has established, not outside it.

---

### Q: Why does God allow the Antichrist to succeed? Why doesn't he just stop him?

For the same reason he allowed the prince of Persia to detain Gabriel, the same reason he allowed the exiles to spend seventy years in Babylon, the same reason Job suffered while Satan brought accusations in the heavenly court. God is sovereign, not reactive. His will is not threatened by the Antichrist — it operates through the Antichrist's permitted rise.

The Antichrist's authority is given. Daniel 11:36 says he will be successful "until the time of wrath is completed, for what has been determined must take place." That phrase — what has been determined — puts the Antichrist's entire career within the frame of divine appointment. He is allowed to rise because God's purposes for the final period of history require the conditions his rise creates: the purification of the church, the confrontation of Israel with its Messiah, and the gathering of the nations into a final reckoning.

### Q: What is the difference between tribulation and wrath?

They are not the same thing, and the Bible treats them differently.

Tribulation — including the great tribulation — is persecution. Its author is the Antichrist, empowered by Satan. It is what Satan and his instrument do when they are given the authority to do it. God allows it, but he does not originate it.

Wrath is punishment. Its author is God alone. Wrath is not discipline or pruning or refining — wrath is the execution of judgment against those who have hardened themselves beyond the reach of repentance. Pharaoh's Egypt experienced God's wrath in the plagues. The nations gathered at Armageddon will experience it at the return of Christ.

Paul's statement in 1 Thessalonians 5:9 — that God has not appointed us to wrath — is a statement about the church's protection from divine judgment, not about protection from persecution. The church will face tribulation. It will not face the wrath of God, because that wrath falls on those who have refused the covering Christ provides.

---

### Q: Does the distinction between tribulation and wrath affect the timing of when the Lord returns?

It clarifies the order without fixing the exact timing. We know the great tribulation runs for three and a half years from the abomination of desolation. We know the rapture — the gathering of the elect — occurs at the Lord's visible return. We know the Lord returns before the end of the seven-year period. What we do not know is exactly when within the great tribulation he returns. Jesus says no one knows the day or hour.

What the tribulation-versus-wrath distinction tells us is this: the church is not removed before the great tribulation starts. The gathering occurs at Christ's return, which follows the abomination of desolation and the cosmic signs. But between the abomination of desolation and the return, the Lord's arrival removes his people before his full wrath is poured out on the unrepentant. The two events — the gathering of the elect and the pouring out of divine wrath — are distinct but occur within the same return of Christ.

### Q: What are the 1290 and 1335 days in Daniel 12, and what do they mean?

The 1260 days — time, times, and half a time — is the duration of the great tribulation. That timeline ends when the Antichrist is thrown into the pit (Revelation 19:20).

The 1290 days and 1335 days extend beyond 1260, placing them in the early period of the millennial kingdom. The text does not specify what these dates mark. Most likely they correspond to significant transitional events in the post-tribulation period — possibly the reconsecration of the temple for the Lord's reign, and the Lord himself entering the temple to reign from Jerusalem. The blessing pronounced on those who reach the 1335th day suggests a landmark of extraordinary significance — a moment of arrival into something the saints have been waiting and suffering for.

Daniel is told the words are sealed. The full meaning of these dates will likely be apparent only when the events themselves arrive.

---

### Q: Will the church be present during the tribulation, or is this just about the Jews?

Both. The distinction the text maintains is that the specific promise of a divinely prepared place of refuge — the escape route to the east described in Daniel 11:41 and Revelation 12:14 — is directed at Israel, specifically the Jews in Judea who are present when the abomination of desolation occurs. The 144,000 sealed servants in Revelation are Jewish. The place of refuge in the desert is prepared for the woman, which is Israel.

But Revelation 12:17 is explicit that Satan's war extends beyond Israel: he goes after "the rest of her offspring — those who obey God's commandments and hold fast their testimony about Jesus." That is the church. The great tribulation targets both. The specific protective provisions operate differently for each.

---

### Q: Where might the place of refuge for the Jews be located during the tribulation?

The text does not identify it precisely. It describes a place in the desert, in the region of present-day Jordan and possibly northern Saudi Arabia — the territory of ancient Edom, Moab, and Ammon that Daniel 11:41 identifies as falling outside the Antichrist's control. Many have speculated that Petra — the ancient city carved directly into sandstone cliffs in southern Jordan — fits the description, being both geographically in the correct region and remarkably defensible. Whether that identification is correct is speculation. The text establishes the region and the providential protection; the specific location remains open.
