# The War Behind the War
### Daniel 10–12 and Revelation 12–13

---

## 1. Setting the Scene: Daniel 10

The vision that opens Daniel 10 arrives in the third year of Cyrus, king of Persia. Daniel — still in Babylon, now under Medo-Persian rule — fasts and prays for three weeks before receiving a response. The reason for the delay, and what it reveals about the nature of spiritual warfare, is the first major subject of this session.

The figure who appears to Daniel on the bank of the Tigris River is described with language that places him immediately in the category of heavenly being: dressed in linen, his face like lightning, his eyes like flaming torches, his arms and legs gleaming like burnished bronze, his voice like the sound of a multitude. The men with Daniel cannot see the figure, but the terror that rolls off the encounter drives them to flee and hide. Daniel alone remains, and even he collapses. This is [[Glossary#Gabriel|Gabriel]], the angelic messenger who consistently serves as the revealer throughout Daniel's visions.

Gabriel's explanation for his delayed arrival is one of the most important passages in Scripture on the mechanics of spiritual warfare at the national and cosmic level:

> *"Since the first day that you set your mind to gain understanding and to humble yourself before your God, your words were heard, and I have come in response to them. But the prince of the Persian kingdom resisted me twenty-one days. Then Michael, one of the chief princes, came to help me, because I was detained there with the king of Persia."*
>
> Daniel 10:12–13

---

## 2. The Authority Matrix: Principalities and National Sovereignty

Daniel 10 is one of the few passages in all of Scripture where the mechanics of spiritual authority over nations — what Paul would call [[Glossary#Principalities|principalities]] — become directly visible. To understand what is happening, the framework from [[i. The Divine Council and the Seventy Nations|Chapter 06]] must be held in view.

At the Tower of Babel, God divided the nations and assigned governance of each to members of the divine council. These angelic beings — seventy in number corresponding to the seventy nations of Genesis 10 — are the principalities operating behind every geopolitical entity. They are not metaphors. They are created beings with real delegated authority over real territories and peoples.

The "prince of Persia" who resisted Gabriel for twenty-one days is precisely this: the angelic principality assigned to govern the Persian Empire. His domain is Persia. Everything that transpires within Persia — including the decisions of the human king of Persia — operates within his sphere of authority.

This raises the question the session presses directly: why could Gabriel not handle this principality alone? The answer lies in jurisdiction. Gabriel was coming to tell Daniel what was going to happen inside Persia — specifically, that the Persian king was about to release the Jewish exiles and send them home. That release was an act of God's sovereign will. But it was going to take place within territory that the prince of Persia had authority over. From the prince of Persia's standpoint, releasing the Jews worked against his interests and against his plans for the empire he governed.

What Gabriel needed was not simply more raw power. He needed authority that covered the domain in question. [[Glossary#Michael|Michael]] — the archangel assigned to Israel — provided exactly that. The Jews who were about to be released were under Michael's authority. Michael's presence added the necessary counterweight: the authority of the angelic prince of Israel standing against the authority of the angelic prince of Persia. The king of Persia, standing between these two competing zones of angelic authority, would act in accordance with God's will because the appropriate authority was in place to ensure it.

> *"Then Michael, one of the chief princes, came to help me, because I was detained there with the king of Persia."*
>
> Daniel 10:13

Daniel 10:21 makes the layered relationship explicit: Gabriel describes Michael as "your prince" — the prince belonging to Daniel's people, Israel.

An important implication follows from this framework. If the principalities operate within lines of divine authority — if their power is delegated and bounded — then human beings do not hold authority to override them. Believers contend against principalities (Ephesians 6:12), but they cannot unilaterally revoke the authority these beings have been given. That authority was granted by God and can only be checked or overridden by the appropriate higher authority operating within God's own structure. This is not a deficiency in the believer's standing. It is simply the nature of a governed universe in which God operates through legitimate channels, not arbitrary power.

The same principle governs the [[Glossary#Antichrist|Antichrist]]. God will permit the Antichrist to rise, to consolidate power, and to wage war against the saints — not because God is helpless to stop him, but because his sovereign will, at this appointed time, is to allow it. The Antichrist's authority is given. When the time of wrath is completed, it will be revoked.

---

## 3. The Prince of Greece

As Gabriel continues speaking, he states that once his work in Persia is finished, the prince of Greece will come (Daniel 10:20). This is not merely a prophecy about Alexander the Great. It is a description of how angelic focus shifts with geopolitical primacy. The principality that governs whichever empire holds dominant authority over the known world is the principality that becomes most consequential in the cosmic contest. When Medo-Persia falls to Greece, the prince of Greece becomes the primary counterpart to the angelic forces aligned with God's purposes.

Gabriel's focus follows accordingly.

## 4. The Author's Lens: How Daniel 11 Works

Daniel 11 contains no fewer than thirty-eight verifiable prophetic fulfillments within its historical sweep — a span running from the third year of Cyrus to the desecration of the Jerusalem Temple in 167 BC. But the structure of the chapter is as important as its content, because the structure reveals what the Lord wants the reader to focus on.

The chapter opens with a compressed account of two centuries of Persian history in two verses. Three more Persian kings will arise, and then a fourth — Xerxes, whose wealth and ambition will stir the entire world against Greece (Daniel 11:2). That fourth king's confrontation with the fragmented Greek city-states is what catalyzes their unification and ultimately produces Alexander the Great.

Then the chapter shifts gear entirely. The next seventeen verses cover approximately 150 years of Greek history in close detail. The author is no longer compressing. He is zooming in. And within those seventeen verses, he barely pauses on Alexander himself — he goes almost immediately to the fracturing of Alexander's empire into four successor kingdoms and then to the two that matter: the [[Glossary#King of the North|king of the north]] and the [[Glossary#King of the South|king of the south]].

The king of the south is Egypt, governed by the [[Glossary#Ptolemies|Ptolemies]]. The king of the north is the Seleucid kingdom, based in what is now Syria and Turkey. Between them, geographically and strategically, sits Israel. The whole of Daniel 11:5–20 recounts their decades-long conflict: alliances made and broken, daughters given in marriage for political ends, armies sweeping back and forth across the landscape, betrayals from within and invasions from without. The character that emerges is one of relentless intrigue, mutual deception, and the grinding brutality of dynastic competition. This is not sanitized history.

The funnel narrows further. After seventeen verses on 150 years, the next eleven verses cover eleven years — and one man.

## 5. Antiochus IV Epiphanes: The Antitype of the Antichrist

Verse 21 introduces him this way:

> *"He will be succeeded by a contemptible person who has not been given the honor of royalty. He will invade the kingdom when its people feel secure, and he will seize it through intrigue."*
>
> Daniel 11:21

Antiochus IV Epiphanes rose to the Seleucid throne not through legitimate succession but through political maneuvering. He was a man of contempt, not honor — yet he was extraordinarily capable and dangerous. The verses that follow trace his campaigns against Egypt, his alliance-making and treaty-breaking, and his repeated encounters with the king of the south. The portrait throughout is of a ruler who achieves through deception what he cannot achieve through direct confrontation, who corrupts those who should be his enemies from the inside, and who uses apparent friendship as a weapon.

At verse 28, his orientation shifts:

> *"The king of the north will return to his own country with great wealth, but his heart will be set against the holy covenant."*
>
> Daniel 11:28

Israel is in the way — geographically, and increasingly theologically. Antiochus moves against the temple establishment, installing corrupt priests aligned with his own agenda. Then, in verses 31 and following, he does what the passage has been building toward:

> *"His armed forces will rise up to desecrate the temple fortress and will abolish the daily sacrifice. Then they will set up the abomination that causes desolation."*
>
> Daniel 11:31

The desecration under Antiochus Epiphanes in 167 BC included the slaughter of a pig on the altar of burnt offering, the erection of an altar to Zeus within the temple precincts, and the complete halt of the sacrificial system. This is the historical event that the Jewish festival of Hanukkah commemorates — the Maccabean revolt that followed, the expulsion of Antiochus's forces from the temple, and the rededication of the sanctuary to the Lord.

The comparison to the [[Glossary#Antichrist|Antichrist]] is explicit and structural: Antiochus [[Glossary#Typology|typologically]] prefigures the Antichrist's actions. But the distinctions are equally important. Antiochus set up an idol — Zeus — in the temple. The Antichrist does something categorically beyond this: he does not install a statue of a god. He declares himself to be God, and demands worship (Daniel 11:36). That is the line Antiochus never crossed. He desecrated the temple in a recognizable pattern. The Antichrist fulfills the pattern in a form that exceeds its historical preview.

---

## 6. The Transition: Verse 35 and the Pivot to the End

Verse 35 is the hinge:

> *"Some of the wise will stumble, so that they may be refined, purified, and made spotless until the time of the end, for it will still come at the appointed time."*
>
> Daniel 11:35

Everything described in Daniel 11 up to this point — the wars between north and south, the rise of Antiochus Epiphanes, the desecration of the temple, the Maccabean resistance — all of it has already happened. But this verse announces that there is still a future time, an appointed time, toward which history is moving. The Antiochus episode is not the end. It is the foreshadowing.

Beginning with verse 36, the subject changes. The "king" described here is no longer Antiochus Epiphanes:

> *"The king will do as he pleases. He will exalt and magnify himself above every god and will say unheard of things against the God of gods. He will be successful until the time of wrath is completed, for what has been determined must take place."*
>
> Daniel 11:36

The jump is intentional and without transition. The author moves directly from Antiochus to the Antichrist — from a historical figure to a prophesied end-times figure — using the same linguistic thread. This is a telescoping technique in prophetic literature: a near-term fulfillment and a far-term fulfillment are presented in sequence because the first is the pattern for the second. The Antiochus material teaches the reader what to recognize. The Antichrist material describes the final version of what was previewed.

This is also why Jesus, in Matthew 24, tells his disciples: *"When you see the abomination of desolation spoken of by the prophet Daniel, let the reader understand."* He is pointing them back to Daniel 11 and 12. The fulfillment Antiochus brought was not final. There is another one coming.

The Antichrist described in Daniel 11:36–45 operates by a different theological principle than his predecessors. Previous kingdoms honored gods — even corrupt ones. The Antichrist honors no god at all, except what the text calls "a god of fortresses" (Daniel 11:38) — a phrase that points toward the full emergence of Satan himself into the open. Behind every system of false worship in human history, behind the principalities and the idols and the power structures, the ultimate agenda has always been to elevate Satan above the Most High God. In the Antichrist, that agenda becomes explicit. Satan is no longer operating behind the scenes through intermediary systems. He is stepping forward.

## 7. The Antichrist's Wars and the Nations Surrounding Israel

Daniel 11:40–43 describes the Antichrist's end-times military campaigns. The same language as before — king of the south and king of the north — reappears, but now applied to end-times actors in the same geographic theater. Egypt will challenge the Antichrist. He will conquer it, along with Libya and what the text calls the Nubians (present-day Sudan). These three nations correspond to the "three kings" the Antichrist subdues in the earlier Daniel visions (Daniel 7:8, 24), providing the explanatory detail those visions left implicit.

Verse 41 notes that Edom, Moab, and the leaders of Ammon will be delivered from his hand — the territories of present-day Jordan and part of Saudi Arabia, lying to the east of Jerusalem. This is not geographically incidental. When Jesus tells those in Judea to flee at the onset of the abomination of desolation, the region he is effectively pointing them toward is precisely this area — the one the Antichrist does not control. *The escape route is built into the geography of the prophecy itself.*

By verse 45, the Antichrist has pitched his royal tents "between the seas at the beautiful holy mountain." The two seas are the Mediterranean and the Dead Sea. The holy mountain is Jerusalem. He has consolidated his victories to the south and east, beaten back challenges from the north, and is now positioned immediately outside Jerusalem. His next move is the temple.

## 8. Daniel 12: Michael Arises

Chapter 12 opens at exactly this moment:

> *"At that time Michael, the great prince who protects your people, will arise."*
>
> Daniel 12:1

Michael does not arise here to stop the Antichrist. He arises because something is about to happen in the heavenly realm that corresponds to the Antichrist's move on the temple. Daniel is told what follows but not why Michael rises at precisely this moment. That explanation is sealed until Revelation.

The very next clause after Michael's arising announces a "time of distress such as has not happened from the beginning of nations until then." This is the [[Glossary#Great Tribulation|great tribulation]] — the three-and-a-half-year period of maximum persecution that begins when the Antichrist stands in the temple, declares himself God, and demands worship. The sequence is tight: Antichrist pitches tents outside Jerusalem → Michael arises → great tribulation begins. What connects the first and the third is what the second is doing.

Daniel is then told of a deliverance: everyone whose name is found written in the book will be delivered (Daniel 12:1). The Lord provides the Jews a place and instructs them to flee. The deliverer is himself the escape route.

## 9. The Timelines: Three and a Half Years, 1290 Days, and 1335 Days

Daniel is given three numerical markers for the end-times period:

"Time, times, and half a time" — one year plus two years plus half a year — resolves to three and a half years, or 1260 days. This is the duration of the great tribulation, from the abomination of desolation to the end of the Antichrist's reign. Revelation confirms this in multiple forms: 1260 days, forty-two months, and time, times, and half a time all refer to the same period.

The 1290 days and 1335 days extend beyond the 1260 and appear to point to events in the early millennial kingdom — possibly the rededication and reconsecration of the temple before the Lord takes up residence in Jerusalem to reign. The text does not explain these precisely, and Daniel is explicitly told that the words are sealed. The blessing pronounced on those who reach 1335 days indicates a specific threshold of significance, but its content remains an open question.

## 10. What Michael Is Doing: Revelation 12 Unlocks Daniel 12

Daniel does not know what Michael is doing when he arises. The answer is not given to him — this is one of the sealed things. But approximately 600 years later, Revelation 12 provides it:

> *"And there was war in heaven. Michael and his angels fought against the dragon, and the dragon and his angels fought back. But he was not strong enough, and they lost their place in heaven. The great dragon was hurled down — that ancient serpent called the devil, or Satan, who leads the whole world astray. He was hurled to the earth, and his angels with him."*
>
> Revelation 12:7–9

This is what Michael rises to do. At the exact moment the Antichrist is pitching his tents outside Jerusalem and preparing to stand in the temple, Michael wages and wins the final battle for the heavenly court. Satan — who since the beginning has retained access to the heavenly realm and used it to bring accusations before God against his people (as seen in Job 1) — is permanently expelled. He loses his place in heaven. The only domain left to him is earth.

The consequences of this expulsion are immediate and catastrophic in both directions. The heavens celebrate (Revelation 12:10–12) — the accuser of God's people is gone. But for the earth, the warning is blunt: *"Woe to the earth and the sea, because the devil has gone down to you! He is filled with fury, because he knows that his time is short."*

Satan, confined to earth, pours everything into his remaining instrument: the Antichrist. With no access to the heavenly court, no further avenue for accusation, no more room for behind-the-scenes maneuvering, he abandons the pretense of working through a merely political figure. He fully inhabits the Antichrist. The Antichrist then steps into the temple and does what Satan has always wanted to do — declares himself God and demands worship. The Antichrist is no longer operating as a human political leader. He has become the fully inhabited vessel of a being who has spent all of history trying to achieve exactly this.

Revelation 12 then describes Israel's flight: the woman — Israel — is given the wings of a great eagle to fly to a place prepared for her in the desert, where she is cared for for 1260 days (Revelation 12:14). This is the same escape route prophesied in Daniel 11:41, in the territory that remains outside the Antichrist's control.

When Satan cannot reach Israel in her place of refuge, he turns against those who remain: "the rest of her offspring — those who obey God's commandments and hold fast their testimony about Jesus" (Revelation 12:17). This is both the remaining Jews who did not flee and the church. This is the great tribulation in its full form.

---

## 11. The Beast Rises: Revelation 13:1–2

Immediately following Satan's failed pursuit of the woman, Revelation 13 opens with a scene that completes the sequence:

> *"The dragon stood on the shore of the sea. And I saw a beast coming out of the sea."*
>
> Revelation 13:1

The sea, in biblical imagery, represents chaos — the realm from which what is monstrous and ungoverned emerges. The beast who rises from it has ten horns and seven heads, matching the imagery from Daniel 7's fourth beast and the kingdoms identified in Revelation 17. This is the Antichrist in his fully manifest form, having been completely possessed by Satan after the expulsion from heaven.

The question of what the [[Glossary#Sea (Biblical Symbol of Chaos)|sea]] symbolizes here is not incidental. It frames the Antichrist's origin — not from the ordered structure of creation, not from legitimate authority, but from chaos itself. He is what rises when Satan has nothing left but a confined theater and a willing human agent.

## 12. Revelation Fills In What Daniel Left Sealed

The relationship between Daniel and Revelation is one of progressive disclosure. Daniel was told that his words were closed and sealed until the time of the end (Daniel 12:4, 9). Revelation is the unsealing. The same events, the same figures, the same periods appear in Revelation — but with the detail that Daniel was not given. The 1260 days appears in Revelation under multiple forms. The principalities identified in Daniel reappear as the powers the Lamb's victory has already overcome. The timelines Daniel glimpsed become the structural framework of John's visions.

This is the pattern the Lord uses throughout Scripture. He does not give new information until the time is right for it. He gives enough to establish the framework, enough to confirm the pattern, and enough to identify the fulfillment when it arrives. This is also why Jesus pointed his disciples back to Daniel rather than giving them a new prophecy. The framework had already been provided. What they needed was not more revelation. They needed to go back and read what had already been given.

The same principle applies now. When the study reaches Revelation in detail, the scaffolding is already up. Daniel built it. Revelation finishes the building.

## 13. The Purpose of Tribulation

The session closes with the question that the material inevitably raises: why does God allow the church and Israel to face the great tribulation? Why does he not simply remove his people from the theater before the storm arrives?

The answer is not comfortable, but it is coherent. God is not primarily interested in comfort. He is interested in character — and in the witness that character under fire produces. The image Daniel himself uses is the crucible: the refining fire burns off what is impure and leaves what is precious. The same metaphor appears throughout Scripture, from the exile prophets to Peter and James in their epistles.

The great tribulation is not God abandoning his people. It is God using the most severe possible pressure to accomplish what cannot be accomplished without it. A faith that has never been tested against loss cannot bear witness to anything. A church that has never been willing to die for what it believes has nothing to say to a world that will demand exactly that.

The testimony that overcomes is described in Revelation 12:11: by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony — because they did not love their lives so much as to shrink from death. That testimony is not possible without the cost that produces it.

---

> **Key Principle:** The great tribulation is neither God's failure to protect his people nor evidence that the Antichrist is stronger than the Lord. It is the appointed context in which the church demonstrates what it actually believes — and in which both Israel and the nations are confronted with the ultimate choice between the one who calls himself God and the one who actually is.
